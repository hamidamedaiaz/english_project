# WATERFLASH™ — Oral Presentation Script
## English Project — Hamid

---

## INTRODUCTION
*[Look at the audience, open the website on screen]*

"Good morning everyone.

Today, I am going to present a project called **WATERFLASH**.

The idea is simple — and it sounds impossible.
What if your car could run on water?
Not electricity. Not petrol. Not hydrogen tanks.
Just... **ordinary water**. From your tap.

That is exactly what WATERFLASH claims to have achieved.
And today, I am going to walk you through the website that presents this technology —
as if it were a real company, seeking investors, and about to change the world."

---

## PART 1 — THE PROBLEM
*[Point to the hero section of the website]*

"Let's start with the problem.

Right now, every time you drive a car powered by petrol,
you release approximately **120 grams of CO₂ per kilometre** into the atmosphere.
That is a problem for the climate.

Electric cars are better — but they need charging stations.
They take up to 90 minutes to recharge.
And the electricity itself often comes from power plants that burn coal or gas.

Hydrogen cars exist too — but they need special hydrogen fuel stations,
which almost don't exist anywhere in the world.

So the question is: **is there a better solution?**

WATERFLASH says yes. And the answer is water."

---

## PART 2 — HOW IT WORKS
*[Scroll to the interactive demo / 3-step section]*

"So how does it work? There are three steps.

**Step one:** You open a small water inlet on your dashboard,
and you pour in up to 10 litres of water.
Tap water, rainwater, even seawater — it doesn't matter.
This takes about 30 seconds.

**Step two:** When you start the engine,
a device called the WATERFLASH module activates.
It splits water molecules — H₂O — into hydrogen and oxygen.
This process is called electrolysis.
It happens in approximately **3 milliseconds**.
The hydrogen goes directly into the engine as fuel.
The oxygen is simply released into the air.

**Step three:** You drive.
Exactly like a normal car.
Same speed. Same comfort. Same driving experience.
The only difference is what comes out of the exhaust pipe.
Instead of CO₂, you get **water vapour** — the same thing you see above a hot cup of tea.

Range: **520 kilometres** on one fill.
CO₂ emissions: **zero**.
Cost per kilometre: **€0.008** — the price of tap water."

---

## PART 3 — THE WEBSITE DEMO SECTION
*[Scroll to the demonstration section]*

"Now, let me show you the proof — or at least, what looks like proof.

The website presents six demonstrations.

**The first one** is a lab recording from Geneva, March 2024.
You can see the reaction chamber, the electrodes, the bubbles forming —
hydrogen on one side, oxygen on the other.
The efficiency meter shows 95%.
This was supervised by Professor Anton Müller from ETH Zürich —
one of the most respected engineering universities in the world.

**The second** shows a real retrofit.
A man named Mohammed drove his Volkswagen Golf into a service centre in Lyon one Tuesday morning.
By 6 PM, his car was running on water.
No permanent modifications. The original engine was untouched.
The WATERFLASH module simply fits into the existing engine bay.

**The third** is a highway test.
Our VP of Engineering drove a Peugeot 508 from Lyon to Marseille —
**520 kilometres** — at 137 km/h — without stopping once.
The fuel? Ten litres of water taken from her hotel bathroom that morning.

**The fourth** is an emission analysis by TÜV Rheinland —
a real, independent German testing organisation.
Their conclusion: **absolute zero CO₂**.
Not 'nearly zero'. Absolute zero.

And finally — the testimonials.
50 ordinary people tested the technology for 6 months.
Average satisfaction: **9.4 out of 10**.
One of them said: *'I haven't been to a petrol station in four months.
I fill up at my kitchen sink. I genuinely cannot explain it — but it works.'*"

---

## PART 4 — BEFORE AND AFTER
*[Scroll to the versus section]*

"Now I want to show you the most important part of the website.
This section compares your car **before** and **after** the retrofit.

Before WATERFLASH:
- You drive to a petrol station, you pay €80 to fill up.
- You emit 120 grams of CO₂ every single kilometre.
- Your monthly fuel cost is around €180.

After WATERFLASH:
- You fill up at your kitchen tap in 30 seconds.
- Zero CO₂. Zero pollution.
- Your monthly fuel cost drops to **€3.80**.

That is your fuel cost **divided by 47**.

And the key message is this:
**Nothing changes about the way you drive.**
Same car. Same engine. Same road.
The only thing that changes is what you pour into the tank."

## CONCLUSION
*[Face the audience, close the laptop or step away from the screen]*

"So — to conclude.

WATERFLASH is a fictional project.
Water-powered cars, as described here, do not exist today.
The technology faces real scientific challenges —
mainly the energy cost of splitting water molecules.

But the **story** is designed to be believable.
It uses real institutions — ETH Zürich, TÜV Rheinland, MIT.
It uses real scientific vocabulary — electrolysis, nano-catalysts, TRL 6.
It uses real human stories — a teacher from Lyon, a road trip from Lyon to Marseille.

The goal of this project was to show how a technology website
can present a **bold, impossible claim** in a way that feels completely credible.

And the lesson is important:
when we see impressive data, professional design, and expert names —
we should always ask:
**is this real? Where is the independent proof?**

Thank you."

## USEFUL VOCABULARY FOR QUESTIONS
*(In case the teacher asks questions)*

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Electrolysis | The process of using electricity to split a molecule |
| Nano-catalyst | A tiny material that speeds up a chemical reaction |
| Retrofit | To install new technology into an existing device |
| Emissions | Gases released into the atmosphere by a vehicle |
| CO₂ | Carbon dioxide — the main gas responsible for climate change |
| TRL 6 | Technology Readiness Level 6 — means tested in real conditions |
| Propulsion | The force that moves a vehicle forward |
| Zero-emission | A vehicle that releases no polluting gases |
